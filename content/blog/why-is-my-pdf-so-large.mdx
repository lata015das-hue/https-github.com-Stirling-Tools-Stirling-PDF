# Why Is My PDF File So Large? (And How to Fix It)

> **Short answer.** Your PDF is large because it contains one or more of: high-resolution images, embedded fonts, form field state, annotations, or hidden blank pages. Fix it by running [Compress PDF](/tools/compress-pdf) at Low or Medium, after removing the bloat sources below.

A 3-page letter shouldn't be 15 MB. When it is, the cause is almost always something the authoring tool embedded silently — not the text itself. Text is tiny. Everything else is the problem.

## The 5 most common causes

### 1. High-resolution images

A single 300-DPI photo at full page size is 8–25 MB. If your PDF has scanned pages or embedded photos, that's your culprit.

**Fix:** Run [Compress PDF](/tools/compress-pdf) at Medium. For scanned docs, [OCR PDF](/tools/ocr-pdf) first — it lets the compressor drop image resolution without losing the text.

### 2. Embedded fonts (too many)

Every font family used in the document gets embedded in full or as a subset. A document using 6 font families can carry 2–4 MB of font data.

**Fix:** Go back to the source and reduce to 2 font families. If you can't, compression alone won't help much — fonts are already compressed internally.

### 3. Form field state

A filled PDF form keeps both the blank template and all filled data. Two copies of every page, essentially.

**Fix:** [Flatten PDF](/tools/flatten). Flattening burns the form fields into the page content and discards the editable state.

### 4. Annotations and revision history

Comments, highlights, sticky notes, tracked changes — all stored separately from the page content.

**Fix:** [Remove Annotations](/tools/remove-annotations) strips them.

### 5. Blank pages

Some exporters add a trailing blank page. It takes space.

**Fix:** [Remove Blank Pages](/tools/remove-blanks) detects and removes them.

## The diagnosis → fix workflow

1. Check the file size and page count. More than ~200 KB/page for text-heavy content means bloat.
2. Open in a reader — are there images? scans? forms?
3. Apply the relevant fix(es) from above.
4. Run [Compress PDF](/tools/compress-pdf) as the final step.
5. Compare sizes. Repeat if needed at a higher compression level.

## Frequently asked questions

### Why is my 1-page PDF 10 MB?

Almost certainly a full-page image — either a scan or an embedded photo. Run [Compress PDF](/tools/compress-pdf) at High, or re-export the image at lower resolution before embedding.

### Does compression reduce quality?

At Low and Medium: imperceptible for most documents. At High: yes, images may look slightly softer at full zoom. Text is never affected.

### Will flattening my form make it uneditable?

Yes. That's the point — you're discarding the editable state to save space. Keep the original if you need to edit later.

### Can I undo compression?

No. Compression is a one-way transformation. Always keep a backup of the original before compressing.

### Is there a way to see what's making my PDF large?

Advanced PDF inspection tools (like `pdfinfo`, `qpdf --show-object`) can break down size by stream type. For most users, the 5 causes above cover 95% of cases without needing to inspect internals.

## Wrap-up

A large PDF is usually an image, font, or form problem — not a text problem. Diagnose which one applies, fix it with the targeted tool, then compress. Start with [Compress PDF](/tools/compress-pdf) and add [Flatten PDF](/tools/flatten), [OCR PDF](/tools/ocr-pdf), or [Remove Annotations](/tools/remove-annotations) as needed.
